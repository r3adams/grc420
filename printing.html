Lithography

  • Used for commercial printing (brochures, pamphlets, booklets) and publication printing (magazines, catalogs) when less than a million copies are required.
  • Requires oil-based ink with high degree of stickiness (tack) and resistance to flow (viscosity), along with aqueous fountain solution that must be monitored for pH and electrical conductivity.
  • Uncommon in packaging because of the high-tack, oil-based ink.
  • Can use soy-based ink for food and cosmetics containers.

Flexography

  • Common for packaging substrates when low-tack, low viscosity water-based ink is required to avoid toxicity.
  • Can print a wide variety of substrates including all types of board, paper, plastic, and metal.
  • Quality has increased to rival lithography due to technical advances in plates and imaging.

Gravure

  • Used when over 1 million copies are required.
  • Very high preparation costs due to expensive imaging and cylinder processing equipment.
  • Very high quality.

Digital

  • Used for prototyping and very short run labels and thin board.
